The Church got there first: eight centuries of marriage preparation

Long before anyone ran a longitudinal study, the Catholic Church was building systems to slow couples down before the wedding. The tools were different, but the core insight was the same one behind this project: the time to look hard at a marriage is before it starts.

Banns: the community as a screening instrument

Start in 1215. The Fourth Lateran Council, one of the most consequential church councils of the Middle Ages, made the reading of marriage banns a requirement across the Latin Church. Before a couple could marry, their intention had to be announced publicly in their parish, traditionally on three successive Sundays, so that anyone who knew of an impediment could come forward.

Strip away the medieval vocabulary and look at the mechanism. The banns were a distributed background check. Is one party already married? Are the two secretly related? Is someone underage, coerced, or already promised elsewhere? No central registry existed that could answer those questions, but the parish community collectively held that information. The banns turned the community into the database.

The problem the banns attacked was clandestine marriage: couples marrying in secret, with no witnesses, sometimes discovering later that the marriage was invalid, bigamous, or contested. A secret marriage is one nobody can sanity-check. The Church's answer was structural: force the decision into daylight and give the people who know the couple a formal channel to object.

Trent: witnesses, form, and the end of the secret wedding

Three and a half centuries later the Council of Trent went further. Its 1563 decree on marriage (known by its opening word, Tametsi) required that a valid marriage take place before the parish priest and witnesses. Marriage stopped being something two people could simply declare to each other in private and became a public act with a required form.

Alongside the public form, canon law developed the prenuptial investigation, which survives to this day. Before a Catholic wedding, the priest is required to establish that both parties are free to marry, that they understand what they are undertaking, and that nobody is being pressured. In practice this means a structured interview, often conducted with each party separately. Freedom of consent is checked deliberately, one person at a time, precisely because coercion hides in joint conversations.

That detail deserves attention. The insight that you learn different things when you separate the partners is the same one behind the write-alone steps in modern assessment design, including ours. The Church arrived at it through centuries of pastoral experience with forced and manipulated marriages.

The waiting period as a feature, not a nuisance

Between the banns, the investigation, and the required form, the medieval and early modern Church had effectively built a mandatory waiting and vetting period into marriage. A couple could not simply decide on Friday and marry on Saturday. The process imposed weeks of delay by design, and the delay itself did work: impulsive matches cooled, objections surfaced, and families had time to raise concerns.

Modern dioceses have kept this instinct. Many today ask couples to begin formal preparation six months or more before the intended wedding date. Whatever one thinks of the theology, as behavioral architecture this is sound: it moves the serious examination of the relationship to a point where the couple can still act on what they find, which is the entire argument for pre-engagement assessment.

Pre-Cana: marriage preparation becomes a curriculum

The twentieth century turned screening into education. In the 1940s, a movement of retreats and conferences for engaged and married couples spread through the American church, taking its name from the wedding at Cana in John's gospel. Out of it grew what most American Catholics now know as Pre-Cana: structured marriage preparation covering finances, children, conflict, in-laws, faith, and expectations, delivered through parish courses, sponsor couples, or weekend retreats.

The topic list is worth noticing. Money, children, family boundaries, how you fight: Pre-Cana converged on roughly the same conversation topics that longitudinal research would later identify as the load-bearing ones. The Church got there through accumulated pastoral experience of watching marriages struggle, which is its own kind of longitudinal dataset, gathered one parish at a time.

FOCCUS: the Church builds a validated instrument

Then the Church did something surprising: it built one of the standard scientific instruments. FOCCUS (Facilitating Open Couple Communication, Understanding and Study) was developed in the 1980s at the Archdiocese of Omaha as a structured premarital inventory. Each partner responds separately to a bank of statements; the tool then maps agreements and disagreements to guide a facilitator through the couple's specific growth areas.

FOCCUS was validated psychometrically, has been through multiple editions, and published five-year follow-up research found it predicted marital outcomes for most couples at rates comparable to the leading secular instrument. It is used far beyond Catholic parishes. A tool built by a diocese for pastoral use became part of the scientific literature this project stands on.

What the tradition understood, and what it could not see

Eight centuries of Catholic marriage preparation encode several insights that modern research has essentially confirmed:

  • Deliberateness beats drift. Marriage is treated as a decision to be examined, not a current to be carried along by. The research version: couples who slide through relationship transitions without deciding do worse than couples who decide.
  • Structure helps. A required process, with a facilitator and a timeline, gets couples to have conversations they would otherwise postpone indefinitely.
  • Outside eyes catch what insiders miss. From banns to sponsor couples, the tradition never treated the couple as the only source of information about the couple.
  • Timing matters. All of it happens before the wedding, on the theory that this is when the knowledge is still actionable.
  • Separate the partners to get the truth. The one-at-a-time canonical interview anticipates the write-alone protocol by centuries.

And there are things the tradition could not see or would not say. The historical system was built to verify validity, not compatibility: it asked whether the marriage could lawfully happen, not whether it was likely to go well. It assumed marriage was permanent regardless, so prediction was beside the point. It had no way to observe conflict behavior, no concept of attachment or of the demand/withdraw cycle, and for most of its history it treated the woman's consent as necessary while doing little about the economic coercion that made refusal unrealistic. Modern preparation, religious or secular, works with tools the tradition simply did not have.

The lineage

It is easy to present relationship science as a break with the past: finally, data instead of doctrine. The honest story is more like a relay. The Church established that marriage deserves a formal examination period, built the social machinery to enforce one, converged on the right conversation topics through sheer pastoral repetition, and eventually produced a validated instrument of its own. Longitudinal research then added what tradition could not: behavioral prediction, measured outcomes, and a way to test which parts of the received wisdom were actually true.

This project sits in that lineage, on the scientific side of the relay, and borrows the tradition's best structural idea without requiring any of its theology: look hard, look early, look together and separately, and let someone outside the couple help you see.
