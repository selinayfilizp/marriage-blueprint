# The Marriage Blueprint, Methodology & Research Rationale (v0.1)

This document explains every structural decision in the assessment: why each dimension exists, why the test has the shape it has, how scoring works, and where the evidence is strong versus provisional. It is written to be handed to a psychometrician, a clinical advisor, or an investor doing diligence.

---

## 1. The core problem with existing tools

The established premarital instruments, PREPARE/ENRICH, RELATE, FOCCUS, are good. They achieve roughly 80% accuracy predicting marital outcomes over five-year follow-ups (Williams & Larson, 1995; Fowers & Olson). But they share three structural limitations:

1. **They are pure self-report surveys.** They measure what couples *believe* about their communication, not how they actually behave under disagreement. Gottman's entire research program demonstrated that observed behavior during conflict carries predictive signal that self-report misses.
2. **They score context-blind.** Ross, Karney, Nguyen & Bradbury (2019) showed, replicated across two longitudinal samples of 515 and 431 couples, that the demand/withdraw pattern predicts *lower* satisfaction for higher-income couples but *more stable* satisfaction for lower-income couples. The same behavior means different things in different circumstances. No existing instrument adjusts for this.
3. **They are positioned post-engagement.** By the time most couples encounter FOCCUS or PREPARE/ENRICH (typically via clergy or a counselor during wedding preparation), sunk costs, social pressure, and announcement momentum bias them against acting on adverse findings. Huston's 13-year PAIR project showed courtship-stage patterns already predict long-term outcomes, the information exists earlier; the instruments just aren't administered earlier.

The Marriage Blueprint is designed to close all three gaps.

## 2. Theoretical backbone: the Vulnerability-Stress-Adaptation model

The assessment's three-layer architecture maps directly onto Karney & Bradbury's (1995, *Psychological Bulletin*) vulnerability-stress-adaptation (VSA) model, the most-cited integrative framework in marital research, with all three components independently confirmed as predictors of relationship satisfaction in a 2022 replication:

| VSA component | Assessment layer | What it captures |
|---|---|---|
| Enduring vulnerabilities | Layer 1 | Attachment, personality, family-of-origin, relationship narrative |
| Stressful events | Layer 2 | Financial architecture, life transitions, external stressors, digital strain |
| Adaptive processes | Layer 3 | Conflict conduct, repair, commitment structure |

The model's central claim: outcomes are not determined by any single factor but by the *interaction*, vulnerable people under high stress with weak adaptation decline; the same vulnerabilities with strong adaptation and manageable stress do fine. This is why the report never reduces to a single score.

## 3. The ten dimensions and their evidence base

### Layer 1, Enduring vulnerabilities

**ATT · Attachment security** (weight 2). Insecure attachment, both the anxiety and avoidance dimensions, negatively predicts relationship satisfaction across a 132-study meta-analysis, with both actor and partner effects. Items are modeled on the ECR (Experiences in Close Relationships) constructs: comfort with dependence, fear of abandonment, comfort with disclosure, deactivating suppression. *For a validated deployment, license or adopt the full ECR-R short form.*

**PER · Emotional stability** (weight 2). Malouff et al. (2010; 19 samples, N=3,848) found four Big Five traits predict intimate-partner satisfaction, low neuroticism, high agreeableness, high conscientiousness, high extraversion, robust across gender and marital status. Neuroticism is the most consistent negative predictor in the longitudinal literature, so v0.1 indexes emotional stability specifically. Openness does not predict satisfaction and is omitted deliberately.

**FAM · Family-of-origin awareness** (weight 1). RELATE (Busby, Holman & Taniguchi, 2001) established family background as one of four distinct predictive contexts. Our items measure *awareness and processing* of family patterns rather than the raw family history itself, the working hypothesis (consistent with the clinical literature but needing our own validation) is that examined patterns are less predictive of harm than unexamined ones.

**HIS · Relationship narrative** (weight 1). Huston's PAIR project (168 couples, 13 years) found courtship dynamics predict (a) divorce vs. stability, (b) satisfaction among those who stay, and (c) speed of dissolution among those who split. Our items probe whether the couple's origin story is one of active choice versus drift, the narrative dimension the PAIR data suggests carries signal.

### Layer 2, Stress landscape

**FIN · Financial alignment** (weight 2). Financial strain is the most common chronic external stressor, and the VSA model shows stress exposure independently predicts outcomes. Items measure transparency (knowing each other's numbers), ease of money conversations, shared goals, and compatible instincts. The *gap* between partners is scored, not just the levels.

**LIF · Life transition readiness** (weight 1). Measures whether dealbreaker-class futures (children, geography, career priority, eldercare) have been explicitly discussed. Low scores here are treated as an "unmapped future" risk factor because unanticipated stressors hit adaptation hardest.

**DIG · Digital boundaries** (weight 1). Partner phubbing shows significant negative associations with relationship satisfaction (r ≈ −.22 to −.27) in a 52-study meta-analysis (N=19,698). No legacy instrument measures this; it is a genuinely contemporary stressor.

### Layer 3, Adaptive capacity

**CON · Conflict competence** (weight 2). Gottman & Levenson (1992) distinguished "regulated" from "nonregulated" couples by the balance of positive to negative affect during conflict; nonregulated couples had triple the divorce rate (19% vs 7%). Items index soft startup, regret-speech, staying-in-the-room, and perspective gain.

**REP · Repair capacity** (weight 3, Tier 1). What distinguishes stable couples is not the absence of negativity but whether de-escalation attempts are made *and accepted*. This is measured twice: by self-report items here and behaviorally in the scenario reflections.

**COM · Commitment structure** (weight 3, Tier 1). Karney & Bradbury's review of 115 longitudinal studies found the satisfaction→stability link is well-replicated but modest; stably happy couples still divorce at ~14% over ten years. Social exchange theory holds that stability is governed by dedication plus barriers and alternatives, so we measure the commitment architecture directly rather than inferring it from satisfaction.

## 4. Why scenarios instead of more survey items (Mode B)

Mode B is the differentiator, and it exists because of a methodological finding: **observed behavior during disagreement predicts outcomes beyond self-report.** Gottman's lab achieved its predictive results by watching couples discuss real disagreements and coding the interaction. That method doesn't scale, it needs trained coders and lab time.

Mode B approximates the observational paradigm in a self-administered format:

1. **Solo write** elicits each partner's uncontaminated natural response, the behavioral analog of Gottman's "startup."
2. **Reveal** creates a genuine (mild) disagreement stimulus: reading your partner's real answer to a charged scenario produces authentic affect in a way survey items cannot.
3. **Guided conversation** is the observational window. The couple has an actual conflict-adjacent conversation, structured by prompts adapted from clinical practice.
4. **Private reflection** replaces the trained observer with structured self-coding: each partner reports, via behaviorally-anchored checkboxes, what *actually happened*, including their own criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling, repair attempts, repair acceptance, and whether the conversation reached a concrete next step.

The six scenarios are not arbitrary: each targets a domain with documented predictive weight (money, values/autonomy, in-law boundaries, children timeline, career sacrifice/relocation, digital attention), and roles alternate so each partner initiates three and responds to three, revealing whether conflict behavior is position-dependent.

**Known limitation:** self-coded behavior is noisier than observer-coded behavior, and subject to social desirability. Mitigations in v0.1: (a) markers are phrased behaviorally and first-person ("I used sarcasm…") rather than evaluatively; (b) reflections are private; (c) both partners' reports are pooled, so one partner's under-reporting is partially offset. The roadmap replaces self-coding with NLP coding of the written responses once validated against human coders (target κ > .70).

## 5. Scoring architecture

**Step 1, Dimension scores.** Each item is 1-5 Likert, reverse-scored where marked, averaged within dimension, and rescaled to 0-100.

**Step 2, Discrepancy analysis.** For every dimension we compute the couple mean *and* the inter-partner gap. Gaps >30 points on weight-2+ dimensions flag as **alert**; gaps >15 flag as **gap**. Rationale: on structural dimensions (money, commitment), the disagreement itself is the risk factor, independent of levels.

**Step 3, Behavioral tallies.** Across 6 scenarios × 2 partners = 12 reflection reports, we count each marker. The composite "horsemen" index weights contempt 3×, reflecting Gottman's finding that contempt is the single strongest behavioral divorce predictor. We also compute the repair-acceptance rate (accepted ÷ offered) and resolution rate (scenarios ending in a concrete next step ÷ 6).

**Step 4, Contextual calibration.** v0.1 collects SES, cohabitation, duration, and children-intent context. The current build uses these lightly (children-intent mismatch feeds the LIF risk narrative). The validated version will implement the Ross/Karney SES moderation: demand/withdraw-like patterns scored as less maladaptive for resource-constrained couples. We ship the hook before the full model because the evidence says context-blind scoring is wrong.

**Step 5, Classification.** Three outputs:

- **Partnership type**, one of seven, grounded in Olson & Fowers' cluster analysis of 8,385 couples (Devitalized 40%, Financially Focused 11%, Conflicted 14%, Traditional 10%, Balanced 8%, Harmonious 8%, Vitalized 9%), renamed for a pre-marital audience (Drifters, Pragmatists, Strivers, Conventionals, Balancers, Harmonizers, Builders). v0.1 assigns types via transparent threshold rules approximating the cluster profiles (e.g., Harmonizers = high emotional composite + low practical composite). The validated version replaces rules with distance-to-centroid against empirically re-derived clusters from our own data. **We deliberately did not adapt the Enneagram:** a 104-sample systematic review (Hook et al., 2021) found its nine types have never been recovered by factor analysis or clustering. Typologies must be derived from data, not imposed on it.
- **Conflict trajectory**, low/middle/high, mirroring Kamp Dush & Taylor's (2012) finding that 20-year conflict trajectories form three stable classes (≈17/61/23%) that couples rarely exit. Assignment uses the horsemen index plus conflict-skill scores.
- **Satisfaction forecast**, stable/declining/at-risk, from a risk-point model (contempt, alert-level gaps, weak commitment, low emotional stability, low repair acceptance, low resolution rate). Framing follows Karney's (2021) heterogeneity finding: 50-90% of couples maintain stable satisfaction, so decline is presented as a minority trajectory to be avoided, not a default to be accepted.

**Step 6, Action plan.** Findings map to a playbook of interventions, each tied to an evidence-based practice (soft startup training, repair rituals, appreciation habits as the contempt antidote, structured future-mapping, financial-therapy referral). Prioritization: contempt first if present, then alert gaps by weight, then low couple scores by weight, with the "15-minute resolution rule" inserted when resolution rate is low. Maximum three actions, behavior change fails when overloaded.

## 6. Design principles

1. **A mirror, not a judge.** The instrument never outputs "don't marry." It surfaces patterns and probabilities; agency stays with the couple. This is both ethically required and clinically wiser, reactance destroys the intervention value of adverse findings.
2. **Trajectories, not verdicts.** Every forecast is framed as changeable, which the intervention literature supports (PREP-style programs show large effects in published trials, though publication bias inflates the average; the German EPL trials showed dramatically lower 11-year dissolution rates for participants).
3. **The couple is the unit of analysis.** Gaps, interaction patterns, and pooled behavior are scored, not two individual grades.
4. **Identity, not score.** The type is the emotional payload, the "that's so us" moment that makes the Enneagram sticky, but ours is anchored to real cluster analysis. Dimensions carry the precision; the type carries the memorability.
5. **Honest about its own epistemic status.** The report states plainly that this is an unvalidated prototype built on validated findings. Overstated precision is the field's besetting sin (popularized "94% prediction accuracy" claims did not survive our adversarial source verification).

## 7. Known limitations of v0.1

- **Unvalidated as an instrument.** The components rest on published research; the assembly does not yet have its own reliability/validity data. That requires the study below.
- **Custom items, not licensed scales.** Items are construct-faithful originals, not the validated ECR-R/BFI/CSI items. Fine for a prototype; a validated deployment should license or adopt the source scales.
- **Self-coded behavior** (see §4).
- **Single-device, sequential flow.** Honesty depends on the handoff protocol being respected. Production version: separate links per partner.
- **WEIRD evidence base.** The foundational studies used predominantly white, middle-class American samples. Cross-cultural validity of both the dimensions and the typology is an open research question, flagged, not solved.
- **No clinical routing.** A production instrument needs screening and referral logic for intimate partner violence and coercive control, which self-report compatibility tools can mask. This is the most important safety gap before any real deployment.

## 8. Validation roadmap

1. **Content validity review**, 3-5 relationship researchers/clinicians review item-construct mapping.
2. **Pilot (n≈100 couples)**, item analysis, internal consistency (target α ≥ .75/dimension), floor/ceiling checks, completion analytics.
3. **Structure study (n≈500 couples)**, confirmatory factor analysis of the 10-dimension model; latent profile analysis to re-derive couple types from our own data (expect the seven Olson-like clusters; accept what the data says).
4. **NLP coding study**, LLM coding of scenario texts vs. 3 trained human coders (target κ > .70); replaces self-coding if achieved.
5. **Longitudinal criterion study**, follow assessed couples 3-5 years; test whether types, trajectories, and forecasts predict engagement decisions, satisfaction (CSI-16), and dissolution.
6. **Moderation studies**, implement and test SES and cultural calibration against outcome data.

## 9. Source basis (verified findings)

- Karney & Bradbury (1995), *Psychological Bulletin*, VSA model; 115-study review; satisfaction→stability link modest.
- Gottman & Levenson (1992), *JPSP*, regulated/nonregulated couples; cascade model; Four Horsemen; contempt as strongest predictor.
- Huston et al., PAIR project, courtship predicts 13-year outcomes.
- Karney (2021), *Annual Review*, satisfaction-trajectory heterogeneity (50-90% stable).
- Ross, Karney, Nguyen & Bradbury (2019), SES moderation of demand/withdraw (replicated).
- Kamp Dush & Taylor (2012), three stable 20-year conflict trajectories.
- Malouff et al. (2010), Big Five meta-analysis (N=3,848).
- 132-study attachment meta-analysis, insecurity → lower satisfaction (actor and partner effects).
- Hook et al. (2021), *J. Clinical Psychology*, Enneagram lacks factor-analytic/cluster support (104 samples).
- Olson & Fowers, ENRICH cluster analysis, 8,385 couples, seven empirical types.
- Busby, Holman & Taniguchi (2001), RELATE four-context framework.
- Williams & Larson (1995), FOCCUS 5-year predictive validity (~80%).
- Phubbing meta-analysis (52 studies, N=19,698), negative satisfaction association.
- Stanley/Markman PREP trials incl. Army RCT (662 couples), pre-commitment timing effects; German EPL 11-year dissolution differences. *Caveat: a meta-analysis including unpublished studies suggests publication bias inflates premarital-education effects.*

Claims that failed adversarial verification and were **excluded** from the design: Gottman's popularized ">90% prediction accuracy"; the "5.6 vs 16.2-year two-trajectory divorce timeline"; specific neuroticism effect sizes attributed to the 115-study review.
